Non-Visual Paper Prototyping

First of all, we're going to develop a paper prototype and then we're going to try and test it on a user. Developing that paper prototype is something that we can do on our own. That's a kind of internal stage. All the work we've done so far in developing our app has been internal. The second stage will be the first time we've taken our app to an external phase to have a user looks at it.

For my test, I took several sheets of paper all the same size and made each represent a decision the user had to make based on the user interaction chart I'd already developed. We're basically taking something very complex apart in order to examine the individual pieces more slowly.

One of the benefits of working in this rough and ready way is that it's very, very fast for you to make changes or expansions to your map, and we're still primarily, just working with language here. I drew an image on my branding screen, but apart from that, the only graphic elements in the other screens are a few boxes to indicate hierarchy and a few dots and lines to separate pieces of content. Any graphic elements should be supporting actors, they shouldn't be taking the lead role. Keeping your communication focused on just text allows the user to imagine any visual form they want to, or imagine no visual form at all. What's important is that you keep the user focused on decision-making.

Don't be afraid if things change from your flowchart that you've already made. You can always make different variations for certain screens and try those out. Nothing is fixed at this stage in the process, so you still have the ability to allow things to change. You have the ability to learn things as you're making. Laying things out like this might make you come up with some new ideas or it might make you rethink the structure that you already had. In many ways, you are the first user who's going to test your paper prototype.

Non-Visual User Testing

Think of this simple first test as a precursor to more developed user testing that you would carry out later in the development process with your app. And because we're fairly early in the process of developing our app. When we do this kind of testing, we're really trying to figure out if there are large major problems that we encounter with the user. And maybe things that we didn't anticipate as a designer, that you only get to see when somebody is experiencing your app, and the pathways of your app for the first time.

You can have your user sit across the table from you. You can hand them your sheets of paper, one at a time, and ask them which option they choose. Depending on what they choose, you can give them the next piece of paper. Remember, we're trying to focus interaction here, we're trying to see how the user interacts with the app. So having some kind of separation, sitting opposite from the user, for instance, can be quite helpful.

The user is playing the role of the user, but you're playing the role of the app. So don't give the user additional explanations of everything that's on a sheet of paper. If they get stuck or ask you a question, you can answer them. But for the most part, you're going to learn a lot more by quietly observing the user interacting with the sheets of paper, and making decisions. You're playing the role of the app here, so if the user needs to enter data into fields, for instance, you can just do that verbally with them.

So you have a constant loop of exchange between user and tester. While that loop is going on, the tester's job is to observe the user to see which decisions they make, but also to see how easy or difficult those decisions are. The user's job is to make those decisions and to give the tester information when needed or to tell them when there's an issue. Both the user and the tester are trying to figure out how the app works. The user is doing this in an active way by making decisions and telling it to the tester. And the tester is doing it in a passive way by observing what the user does.

Ideally, the user won't run into any problems and the tester will really be observing just what works. But it's also really useful when the user finds something that doesn't work. Because then you know it's a problem, and you know what you've got to fix. And if you run this preliminary paper test with a few different users, and they all have the same issue, they all run into a problem in the same place. Then you can be pretty sure that it's actually a genuine problem, and you're going to have to change something with your app.

Try it yourself: Non-Visual Prototyping & User Testing

Now that I've discussed non-visual prototyping and user testing with you, I'd like you to try it out by developing a paper prototype and testing it out on a user yourself. This is a useful exercise to observe user patterns and behavior, and understand where points of confusion may exist for your user. There's no set way to do thisâ€”the best approach is allow the user to make their own decisions as much as possible and allow them to talk through their decisions so you can observe the results.

  1. If you haven't already, develop an interaction map for your app as it will help you develop your paper prototype. Once you've made your map, use it as a reference to guide you in "dismantling" your app onto single sheets of paper.
  1. On another sheet of paper, write down the tasks that users should be able to complete in your prototype. If there is a specific order to the tasks, please note that too.
  1. Recruit a friend or family member to be your test user. Don't worry about the person actually being from your app's target user group, as the point of this activity is to give you some experience with this method of user testing. Of course, if you can find someone who meets the profile of your target group, all the better!
  1. Find a comfortable spot for the test, such as at a table or desk. Seat yourself across from your test user, or beside them. Have your paper "screens" next to you, face down, in a little pile, along with your list of tasks, the list of questions below, and a notepad and pencil for taking notes.
  1. Explain to the user the purpose of the test: you're testing a prototype for an app and you want to observe their thoughts and reactions as they move through the various functions of the prototype. Explain that you will be asking them some questions throughout the process. Encourage the test user to speak out loud as they evaluate each screen of the app and make decisions throughout the test.
  1. Hand the test user one "screen" from your stack and ask them a question or two from the list below. Listen to their answer. Take notes. Observe how the user interacts with the screen before handing them another piece of paper from your stack.

Testing Questions (not an exhaustive list)

  • Before even looking at the prototype, what do you expect to do with this app?
  • What is the first thing you notice on this screen?
  • What is the first thing you would do on this screen?
  • Your task is X. What would be the first thing you would do here to accomplish that task?
  • (after observing the user press a particular part of the interface) Why did you choose that button?
  • Is there anything on this screen that seems out of place?
  • Is there anything missing from this screen?
  • Is the prototype easy to use?
  • How do you feel when you are using the prototype?
  • What would you change about the prototype?
