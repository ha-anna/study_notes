To complete this assignment, you’ll need to submit an idea for an app that solves a problem which you can clearly identify and that you relate to. In your submission, please include:

  • A name, tagline, and description for your app.
  • Define who the users of your app will be.
  • Decide how your app will work.
  • Do research on similar apps.

See the “My Submission” section for the specific prompts you’ll need to answer.

Three of your peers will assess your submission using the following criteria:

  • Did your peer submit the name/title and tagline for their app?
  • Did your peer submit a short paragraph describing their app? Their response should mention what the app does and what problem it solves.
  • How well does your peer’s app idea solve the problem they described?
  • Did your peer describe four specific functions that their app will do?
  • How well are these functions related to solving the problem that your peer's app will solve?
  • Did your peer describe their initial target user group with one short profile description of an ideal user from this group?
  • Did your peer list 4 client/designer goals for their app design?
  • Did your peer name and describe 3 existing apps that are similar to their own idea?
  • Do you have any suggestions for your peer about how they could widen or limit the scope of their app?

What does the app do? List out several functions of the app. Can you determine a hierarchy of function among them? What is the app’s most important or primary function? And then, what are some secondary or less important functions?

Review the app’s most important or primary function. Can you come up with a one-line description of the app based upon it?

How does the app work? Remember that this doesn’t refer to how the app works technically, but how the app goes about achieving its goal.

Who benefits from the app? Who are its potential users?

And finally, why would someone use the app? I’ll be discussing goals and outcomes more specifically in an upcoming video, but in the meantime try to guess what the developer’s goals are for the app.

Try it yourself: Map out the possibilities

At this point in the course, you probably have a few ideas floating around in your head about the app concept you like to work on in this course. Now is a perfect time to develop these ideas further.

Take a moment to map out all the possibilities for your idea.

  • Consider your idea as an amorphous, unfixed entity. It shouldn’t be a clear idea in your mind right now, and that’s OK.
  • Use a “what-if” strategy to build on your idea. In what ways could your idea manifest or expand?
  • Don’t censor yourself—remember that you can always say no to any possibility later.
  • Map your idea out on paper with a pen or pencil.
  • Bounce your idea off of a friend or family member. What’s their response?

If you have several ideas in mind, then just repeat the process until they become more refined. Keep in mind you’re not committing to the single idea your project will be based around yet. Revisit your ideas after you’ve watched the rest of the lesson content for this week.

SEOULseum: Korean history and culture at your fingertips

Seoul (South Korea) is booming with various museums, palaces and historical locations waiting to be visited. However, buying tickets and finding information about museums is time-taking and confusing.

For one, there is the language barrier. While many museums have their English page, more often than not, it is rarely updated. Therefore, finding information about new exhibitions and their ticket prices is poses a challenge.

Second, reserving and buying tickets is also an issue. Every museum has a different way of dealing with the payment, and often it is impossible to buy a ticket without a Korean bank card or a Korean phone number.

The goal is to introduce easy payment solutions to tourists, to have all of the museums and attractions up-to-date information in one place, and to have it in one of the most spoken languages. By having all of the bookings and information in one place, users can save time and organize their trips more efficiently.

  • Let the user reserve and buy tickets.
  • Inform the user about museums, current exhibitions, and other cultural locations.
  • Make a list of museums to visit and already visited museums.
  • Introduce people to travelers/locals culture and history by providing accessible knowledge about museums.

Political:

  • interested in local culture, history, art;
  • interested in seeing the world from a new perspective;

Financial:

  • on a budget;
  • not having a local banking account;
  • not wanting to deal with currency exchanges;

Social:

  • like to visit new places;
  • like to experience a different culture;
  • adventurous;

Functional:

  • save time;
  • having tickets in one place;
  • reduce confusion caused by foreign currency (being worried about paying too much/not enough/spending too much time trying to figure out the bills and coins);
